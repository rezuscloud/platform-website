# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

RezusCloud serves technically literate visitors who want proof more than promises. The primary audience is self-hosters and homelab operators who want to own their infrastructure on their own hardware. The secondary audience is platform engineers, infra leads, and technically curious teams evaluating whether RezusCloud is credible enough to improve or replace parts of their platform stack. They arrive skeptical, curious, and ready to inspect how the system works.

## Product Purpose

RezusCloud turns the personal-cloud thesis into something concrete: own the control plane, run workloads on your machines, and manage infrastructure like a system you can actually inspect and steer. The homepage should make that thesis feel real, understandable, and runnable. Success means visitors quickly grasp the idea, trust that the product is technically serious, want to explore its layers, and leave wanting to try it themselves.

## Brand Personality

Bold, underground, credible. RezusCloud should feel opinionated, hands-on, and mechanically literate, like a tool built by people who actually run systems. The emotional target is curiosity turning into conviction: this is real, I understand it, and I want to poke at it.

## Anti-references

- Static brochure sites that decorate a narrative with fake desktops or props.
- Retro novelty, including toy terminal skins, parody nostalgia, and arcade-style gimmicks.
- Generic enterprise SaaS pages with polished-but-empty gradients, card grids, and category copy.
- Overcomplicated faux OS simulations that prioritize cosplay over clarity or credibility.

## Design Principles

1. Prove through interaction: the interface should demonstrate how the platform works, not merely describe it.
2. Make ownership tactile: every layer should reinforce that infrastructure is something you can inspect, steer, and keep near you.
3. Nostalgia must earn trust: retro computing is a framing device, not the product, and credibility beats costume.
4. Exploration should compound understanding: each interaction should reveal a deeper layer of the system instead of distracting from it.
5. Technical confidence, not enterprise posture: speak like builders showing a working machine, not marketers presenting a category slide.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Ship to WCAG AA as the baseline. Maintain clear semantics, readable contrast, visible focus states, and keyboard-safe interactions. Motion should never be required to understand the experience, and any layered interaction should preserve a meaningful non-animated fallback.
